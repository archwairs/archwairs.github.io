Relaxation Dynamics in a Confining Potential

Numerical and analytical study of stochastic particle motion with dry friction

1. Introduction

Stochastic models of particle motion provide powerful frameworks for understanding transport processes in physics, chemistry, and biology. Among them, the random walk and its continuous counterpart, the diffusion process, form the basis for a wide range of phenomena; from molecular diffusion to active matter dynamics.

In this project, we study a biased random walk in one dimension where, at every timestep, the particle moves either left or right with probabilities that depend on an external bias. The bias is chosen such that the particle has a higher probability of moving toward the origin, effectively creating a restoring tendency. We explore how this bias and the initial position of the particle influence the time evolution of the mean-squared displacement.

Numerical simulations reveal an interesting non-monotonic behavior of the MSD: it initially increases, reaches a maximum (a peak), and then decreases as the particle relaxes toward the origin. The time and height of this MSD peak depend systematically on the model parameters: a stronger inward bias causes the particle to reach the origin faster, leading to an earlier and smaller peak, while starting farther from the origin delays the peak and increases its magnitude.

2. Model

We consider a one-dimensional biased random walker moving in a linear confining potential:

\[V(x) = \alpha |x|\]

which generates an effective drift toward the origin. The corresponding Langevin equation in the viscous limit is given by:

\[\gamma \dot{x} = -\frac{dV}{dx} + f(t) \Longrightarrow \gamma \dot{x} = -\alpha\,\mathrm{sgn}(x) + f(t)\]

where \(f(t)\) is the noise term with the following properties:

\[\langle f(t)\rangle = 0 \quad \text{and} \quad \langle f(t_1)f(t_2)\rangle = 2D\gamma^2\delta(t_1 - t_2)\]

3. Simulation Methodology

We simulate this model by numerically integrating the Langevin equation using the Euler–Maruyama method. From equations (2) and (7) in the PDF, we write:

\[\dot{x} = -\frac{\alpha}{\gamma}\mathrm{sgn}(x) + \sqrt{2D}\,\xi(t)\]

Integrating over the interval \([t, t + \Delta t]\) gives the discretized update rule:

\[x(t + \Delta t) - x(t) = -\frac{\alpha}{\gamma}\mathrm{sgn}\big(x(t)\big)\Delta t + \sqrt{2D\Delta t}\,\eta_n\]

where \(\eta_n \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)\) is a standard Gaussian random variable. This Euler–Maruyama scheme is implemented in the interactive simulation below.

5. Mean-Squared Displacement Analysis

The MSD shows characteristic non-monotonic behavior: diffusion initially increases the spread, but the confining potential eventually pulls particles back toward the origin, causing the MSD to peak and then decay. This behavior illustrates the interplay between drift toward the origin and diffusive spreading.

Observation:

Stronger confinement (larger \(\alpha\)) causes earlier MSD peaks, while larger friction \(\gamma\) affects the peak height. Starting farther from the origin increases both the peak time and magnitude, reflecting greater initial spreading before drift dominates.

6. Probability Density Evolution

The probability density evolves from a delta-like peak at the initial position toward the equilibrium distribution. The theoretical equilibrium distribution for this system is:

\[P_{\mathrm{eq}}(x) = \frac{\alpha}{2\gamma D} e^{-\alpha|x|/\gamma D}\]

This exponential distribution reflects the balance between the confining potential and thermal noise.

Key Features:

  • Initial concentration at \(x = x_0\)
  • Gradual spreading due to diffusion
  • Asymmetric relaxation toward the origin
  • Convergence to exponential equilibrium distribution

7. Results and Discussion

Key Findings:

  1. Non-monotonic MSD: The mean-squared displacement exhibits a characteristic peak before decaying to its equilibrium value, demonstrating the competition between diffusion and drift.
  2. Parameter Dependence:
    • Larger \(\alpha\) (stronger confinement) → earlier and smaller MSD peak
    • Larger initial \(x_0\) → delayed and larger MSD peak
    • Friction \(\gamma\) modulates the relaxation timescale
  3. Probability Density Evolution: The distribution evolves from an initial delta function to an exponential equilibrium distribution.
